Inpatient Antimicrobial Guide

Empiric & culture-directed therapy, dosing, durations, pathogen overviews, Gram/culture guidance, and anatomical (syndrome) pathways — all offline & iPad-ready.
Quick tips
  • Start empiric therapy based on site, severity, Gram-stain and local resistance patterns.
  • De-escalate once cultures and susceptibilities are available.
  • Always document source control decisions (drainage, device removal).
  • Adjust dosing for renal/hepatic dysfunction; monitor levels where required.

Pathogen Overview — Selected Genera & Key Species

Prevalence, risk factors, vectors/transmission, resistance, pathophysiology, and quick clinical notes.
Mycobacterium (TB & non-TB mycobacteria)
Prevalence: Global; TB endemic in many regions.
Risk factors: HIV/immunosuppression, close-contact exposure, crowded living.
Transmission: Airborne droplets.
Resistance: MDR/XDR TB increasing; check sensitivity to rifampin/INH/fluoroquinolones/aminoglycosides.
Pathophysiology: Intracellular survival in macrophages; granuloma formation; chronic disease course.
Clinical notes: RIPE therapy and DOT where indicated; prolonged therapy, monitor LFTs, and use specialist input for resistance.
Corynebacterium
Prevalence: Worldwide; many species are commensals.
Risk factors: Indwelling devices, immunocompromise.
Transmission: Contact/colonization.
Resistance: Species-dependent; some isolates penicillin-resistant.
Notes: C. diphtheriae causes toxin-mediated disease — antitoxin and antibiotics indicated. Non-diphtherial species can cause endocarditis and bloodstream infections in vulnerable hosts.
Nocardia
Prevalence: Environmental (soil), worldwide but opportunistic.
Risk factors: Steroids, transplant, chronic lung disease.
Transmission: Inhalation; direct inoculation for cutaneous disease.
Resistance: Variable; many isolates susceptible to TMP-SMX, but check susceptibility.
Pathophysiology: Weakly acid-fast, filamentous bacteria; disseminate to CNS.
Clinical notes: Long courses (6–12 months); higher doses for CNS involvement.
Streptococcus spp.
Prevalence: Very common — pharyngitis, pneumonia, invasive disease.
Risk factors: Age extremes, comorbidities.
Resistance: Penicillin remains effective for many species; macrolide resistance variable.
Pathophysiology: Capsule & toxins mediate invasiveness.
Clinical notes: Treat based on site; high index for invasive disease (bacteremia/endocarditis).
Staphylococcus aureus
Prevalence: Common colonizer and pathogen (SSTI, bacteremia, endocarditis).
Risk factors: Diabetes, IV devices, recent hospitalization.
Resistance: MRSA (mecA), some VISA/hVISA described.
Notes: Remove infected devices; choose anti-staphylococcal beta-lactams for MSSA; vancomycin/linezolid/daptomycin for MRSA depending on syndrome.
Clostridium spp. (incl. C. difficile)
Prevalence: Environmental & gut flora; C. difficile common nosocomial pathogen.
Risk factors: Recent antibiotics, hospitalization, PPI use.
Transmission: Spore-mediated; fomites.
Notes: Oral vancomycin is first-line for severe C. difficile; fecal microbiota transplantation for recurrent disease (per guidelines).
Bacteroides
Prevalence: Normal gut flora; common in intra-abdominal infections.
Resistance: Beta-lactamase production common — choose agents with anaerobic coverage.
Notes: Source control (drainage) plus metronidazole or beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor therapy.
Prevotella
Prevalence: Oral and upper-airway flora; associated with aspiration pneumonia and oral infections.
Therapy: Metronidazole or beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor; consider polymicrobial coverage.
Actinomyces
Prevalence & Risk: Oral flora; dental disease, poor hygiene; cervicofacial infections common.
Therapy: High-dose penicillin, prolonged courses (months) often required.
Burkholderia cepacia complex
Prevalence: Environmental; important for cystic fibrosis patients.
Resistance: Often multidrug-resistant; combination therapy frequently required.
Notes: Infection control critical in CF centers.
Intracellular Pathogens
Groups: Chlamydia, Mycoplasma, Legionella, Rickettsia, Coxiella.
Therapy: Doxycycline, macrolides, or fluoroquinolones depending on organism. Many are not amenable to standard beta-lactam therapy.
Fungi overview
Important genera: Candida (yeast), Aspergillus (mold), Cryptococcus, Mucorales.
Risk: ICU, central lines, neutropenia, transplant, prolonged antibiotics.
Therapies: Echinocandins for candidemia; voriconazole for invasive aspergillosis; amphotericin for mucorales/cryptococcus induction.
Viruses overview
Common inpatient viruses: HSV-1/2, VZV, Influenza, CMV, RSV.
Approach: Syndrome-directed testing (PCR, antigen); start antivirals in severe disease / high-risk patients.
